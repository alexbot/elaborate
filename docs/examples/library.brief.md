# Bring people back to the library branch

> *"Our local library branch is losing visitors and the city might close it. I want to help revive it."*

## Summary

A neighborhood library branch faces closure over declining foot traffic. A resident with no formal authority wants to save it — but midway through the session reframed the entire project: "I've been working backwards — I never stopped to ask whether the library is actually what the neighborhood needs." The project landed as a research phase that could confirm or kill the original premise.

## Purpose

The respondent entered with a clear idea — bring people back, prove demand, prevent closure — and left with a different project. The advantage holds: packing the library with regular visitors "makes the closure argument fall apart on its own terms" in a way petitions and fundraisers can't. But the measurement split: foot traffic high enough for the city, *and* sustainable because people actually want to be there. "You could give away free coffee and pack the place, but that doesn't mean people actually need the library." The city's threshold is unknown. What people would come for is unknown. Who "people" even are — former regulars or new users — is unknown.

The respondent surfaced a sequencing problem: measurement assumes "people using the library regularly for their own purposes," but purposes haven't been discovered. "What I should have said is: success means identifying what people actually need, *then* building programming around that, *then* watching whether people come back." The project reframed from executing a revival to researching whether one makes sense — accepting that the neighborhood might need "a community center, or job training facility, or after-school program" instead of a library.

## Goals

1. **Establish sustainable library use rooted in genuine patron needs** — Discover what purposes would draw people, since the choice between restoring the old library versus reinventing it remains open and "genuine patron needs" are currently unknown.
2. **Map the city's closure criteria** — Determine foot traffic threshold, measurement method, timeline, and whether other criteria (cost per visitor, alignment with city priorities) matter.
3. **Clarify the target returning-visitor profile** — Decide whether to pursue former regulars, new community members, or both, since each requires different tactics and carries different risk.

## Stakeholders

- **City council** (external) — makes the closure decision using foot traffic; threshold, measurement method, and timeline are all unknown.
- **Library staff** (primary) — day-to-day operators not yet consulted; hold knowledge of space constraints, patron behavior, and what's been tried.
- **Older regulars** (primary) — use the library for quiet study; a redesign toward community hub programming would repel them.
- **Neighborhood residents** (secondary) — needs unknown; the respondent has guesses but calls them "not enough to build on."
- **Respondent** (advocate/catalyst) — resident deliberately scoped to research and advocacy, not decision-making.
- **Schools** (conditional) — deferred until neighborhood research shows whether after-school or literacy gaps exist.

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Neighborhood research — talk to families, schools, social services, job seekers to discover actual gaps
- Interview city council for closure criteria, threshold, and timeline
- Early staff discovery conversations — constraints, patron history, feasibility
- Assess whether the library is the right institutional solution versus alternatives
- Establish restoration-versus-reinvention decision framework after research completes
- Map schools as potential partners; defer formal engagement until evidence shows relevance

**Out of scope:**
- Deciding what the library becomes — deferred to community, staff, and council
- Implementation of programming, staffing, or operational changes
- Negotiations or commitments to the city about future traffic numbers

**Constraints:**
- City budget limits, building condition, and existing contracts — all unknown, all constrain feasibility
- Closure timeline unknown — if imminent, the in-scope research may be infeasible
- Respondent has no formal authority over any stakeholder group

## Uncertainties

**Unresolved tensions:**
- Research could reveal the library isn't what the neighborhood needs — the purpose becomes incoherent, but the respondent accepts this as a valid finding
- The library can't serve both quiet-seeking regulars and activity-seeking families in one space; spatial separation or choosing one group is the only resolution
- Measurement can't precede need discovery — foot traffic targets are meaningless until purpose and user profile are defined
- Unknown closure timeline may not allow the in-scope research

**Assumptions:**
- The respondent's personal preferences are not a proxy for neighborhood needs
- The respondent's role is research and advocacy only; decisions deferred to those with authority
- Foot traffic is meaningless as a metric without first defining what the library offers and for whom

**Findings:**
- Decision sequencing gap: restoration-vs-reinvention is deferred until after research, but the city may ask "what is the library becoming?" before an answer exists
- The respondent's uncertainty about whose needs to prioritize is appropriate — a prioritization framework is needed after research, not before

**Respondent's closing concern:** Remove the assumption that restoration-vs-reinvention "cannot be deferred" — the respondent chose to defer it. The research could reveal the library isn't the right solution, and that's a valid outcome. Closure timeline is a blocking constraint to clarify immediately.
