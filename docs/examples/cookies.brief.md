# Farmers market cookie business

> *"I've been baking cookies for years and my friends love them. I want to start selling them at our local farmers market once a week."*

## Summary

A home baker wants to turn a hobby into a weekly farmers market stall, but hasn't yet checked whether home baking is legal, what the market organizer requires, or whether the economics work. The most consequential finding from the session: the respondent's instinct to "just try it" collides with a hard legal gate they haven't investigated, and resolving that gate may require more than a single conversation with the market organizer.

## Purpose

The respondent wants to start selling cookies at a local farmers market once a week. Friends have been asking to buy for a while, and the farmers market feels like "a natural place to do it." The advantage is existing demand from friends and a ready venue.

On measurement, the respondent initially said success means "having fun doing it and people are excited to buy them" plus not losing money on ingredients and time. They then revised: not just break even, but "actually work as a real thing — actually be sustainable." They weren't sure whether sustainability was different from breaking even or the same thing, and the session didn't resolve that tension.

## Goals

1. **Identify operational constraints for weekly sales** — surface licensing, kitchen, time, and supply chain limits that determine whether weekly operation is feasible at all. The respondent elevated this to first priority: "if I need a commercial kitchen or licenses I don't have, that changes everything."
2. **Validate market demand beyond friends** — test whether farmers market customers (not just the existing friend network) will buy cookies at a viable price point. Friends "would just come by when they feel like it" and are not reliable weekly customers.
3. **Establish a sustainable unit economics model** — determine per-unit cost, pricing, and volume needed to cover booth fees, ingredients, and time. Currently undefined: the respondent "hasn't done the math on what that actually means."

## Stakeholders

**Friends** — occasional supporters, not reliable weekly customers. Would show up "out of support" rather than genuine demand.

**Farmers market customers** — unknown. The respondent hasn't talked to anyone at the market and is "kind of guessing" about what they want. Pricing, flavor preferences, and dietary needs are all unresearched.

**Farmers market organizer** — controls stall access. The respondent hasn't contacted them and doesn't know their rules, whether other bakers already sell there, or whether they'd accept another cookie vendor. *(Respondent)*

**Family** — affected by weekend time and kitchen use. The respondent hasn't discussed the plan with them and is "assuming they'll be fine with weekend baking, but I don't know."

## Scope

**In scope**
- Validate whether farmers market customers want to buy cookies at a sustainable price
- Determine whether home baking is legally permitted for farmers market sales (food safety, licensing, permits)
- Research whether other bakers or cookie vendors already operate at this market
- Model basic unit economics: ingredient costs, time per batch, viable price point, weekly profit/loss
- Assess family's actual willingness and time availability for a weekly weekend operation

**Out of scope**
- Recipe development or flavor testing — production work, not validation
- Full business plan or legal entity structure — depends on validation results
- Dietary-specific options (gluten-free, vegan) — respondent will "see what people actually want before I commit to making it"; only relevant if customers request them

**Constraints**
- Home baking must be legally permitted — "if it's not allowed, that's it, I'm done before I start"
- All baking and prep must fit weekend availability (works during the week)
- Family must be willing to accommodate weekend baking
- Upfront costs (booth fees, equipment, licensing) cannot reach "hundreds of dollars"

## Uncertainties

**Unresolved tensions**
- The respondent treats sustainability and break-even as possibly the same thing, but their goals require sustainability to mean more than break-even. The session surfaced this but didn't resolve it.
- The respondent initially assumed constraint discovery could wait until after a market attempt, then reversed: "maybe that one comes first." The validation phase flagged this as a contradiction — deferring constraints while legality is a hard gate is internally inconsistent.

**Unknowns**
- Whether home baking is legal for farmers market sales under local and state regulations. The market organizer may not know the full regulatory picture — state-level food handler licensing, commercial kitchen requirements, and labeling rules may require direct contact with a health department.
- Whether other bakers already sell at this market and whether the organizer would accept another cookie vendor.
- What farmers market customers actually want — pricing, flavors, dietary needs are all unresearched.
- Whether the respondent's family will agree to the weekend time and kitchen commitment.

**Open respondent concerns**
The respondent ended the session with three unresolved requests: remove dietary preferences from scope (applied), upgrade legality from deferrable to prerequisite (partially captured in goal reordering but not fully reflected in the constraint/assumption relationship), and add a family conversation as a concrete next action.
